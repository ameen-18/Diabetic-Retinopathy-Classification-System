Frequently Asked Questions

These are the questions raised by patients .

  • What is diabetic retinopathy?

    Diabetes affects the small blood vessels in the body and the retina in the eye contains many fine blood vessels. Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a complication of diabetes that affects the back of the eye. When someone has high and varying blood sugar levels, the blood vessels in the retina can become irreversibly damaged. This can lead to loss of vision and eventually blindness.

  • How can you tell if diabetes is affecting your eyes?

    If you have diabetes, your eyes will be affected by it, which is why it is really important to get regular eye health examinations from your eye care practitioner. Diabetic retinopathy commonly occurs in both eyes and most often starts with no visual symptoms. In the early stages, the walls of the blood vessels in the retina weaken. This can cause fluid and blood to leak into the eye, which sometimes results in blurred vision. Some other symptoms which could develop later include fluctuating vision, double vision, floaters, and impaired colour vision.

  • What are the main stages of diabetic retinopathy?

    The four stages of diabetic retinopathy are classified as mild, moderate, severe (nonproliferative), and proliferative.

  • Can lowering blood sugar level help with diabetic retinopathy?

    When blood sugar levels are consistently high, the chances of diabetic retinopathy developing are high. That is why it is important to control your blood sugar levels in order to slow down diabetic retinopathyâ€™s progression to vision loss.

  • What is the cure for diabetic retinopathy?

    While diabetic retinopathy can not be cured, treatments can help prevent or delay vision loss for anyone who has diabetes.
    For people with diabetes, it is critical to receive regular eye examinations as early detection and treatment can help prevent vision loss. These eye health examinations should be done annually or every two years at a minimum by an eye care practitioner.
    People who have suffered from vision loss due to diabetic retinopathy can access rehabilitation services from low vision clinics.
